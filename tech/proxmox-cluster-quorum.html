Proxmox: Clustering, Quorum, and HA Basics

Proxmox becomes a platform when you treat it as a cluster rather than a single node. The cluster gives you a shared view of nodes, guests, storage, and permissions.

What quorum really does

Quorum answers: “Is it safe to make cluster-wide changes right now?”

  • With quorum: safe config changes, migrations, HA actions.
  • Without quorum: nodes can often keep running existing VMs locally, but the cluster avoids risky writes.

Two-node clusters (the tiebreaker problem)

Two nodes can’t form a safe majority if they lose contact. Typical fixes:

  • Add a third Proxmox node (even a tiny one)
  • Use a QDevice to provide an external vote

HA in one paragraph

HA helps restart VMs on healthy nodes during failure, but it depends on:

  • quorum stability
  • storage design (shared storage or replication that matches your recovery expectations)
